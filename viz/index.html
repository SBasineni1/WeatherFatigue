Are Storm Warnings
Actually Verified?

Exploring how often severe weather warnings across the United States are confirmed by actual storm reports — and what that means for public trust.

Every year, the National Weather Service issues thousands of severe thunderstorm and tornado warnings. But how many of those warnings are actually verified by real-world storm reports? The answer varies dramatically by region and forecast office.

This project analyzes over 600,000 local storm reports and 400,000 warning polygons to understand warning verification patterns across the country. The data spans from 2008 to 2025 and covers every NWS Weather Forecast Office in the contiguous United States.

    What Does This Mean?

    The data reveals significant variation in warning verification rates across different NWS offices and regions. Some offices consistently verify over 60% of their warnings, while others fall below 30%.

    These differences matter. When warnings go unverified repeatedly, public trust can erode — a phenomenon known as the "cry wolf" effect. People in areas with lower verification rates may become desensitized to warnings, leading to slower response times during genuine severe weather events.

    Understanding these patterns is the first step toward improving warning systems and maintaining the public trust that saves lives during severe weather.
