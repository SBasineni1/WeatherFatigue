Measuring Weather Fatigue

Have you ever grown tired of weather warnings? Why does it seem like they never verify?

By Suchit Basineni, Shivani Anand, Elinor Tu, Deborah Biru, Jason Kim, Rishik Gowrishetti

Edited by Jenny Williams, Nikhil Chinchalkar

"Weatherman says," Kev scoffed. "I wouldn't trust that silly bugger to know it's raining now."

— Ransom Riggs, Miss Peregrine's Home for Peculiar Children

May 21, 2011 — a storm brews over the Great Plains. Meteorologists from the National Weather Service release word of a supercell thunderstorm and large hail. Yet the next morning — and all day throughout — the perfect conditions for heavy storm development grow, and forecasters become increasingly certain that they have something entirely greater on their hands. By 11:30am on May 22: public, expect severe weather with potential for tornadoes, large winds, and hail. By 1:30pm: a tornado watch is established for southwestern Missouri. And at 5:17pm on May 22, 2011, tornado sirens ring out in Joplin, Missouri, a city bordering Oklahoma and Kansas at the heart of the Great Plains.

No one pays any mind. In Joplin, tornado sirens are a daily occurrence.

19 minutes later, an EF5 tornado — the most severe on the National Weather Service's Enhanced Fujita scale, describing "catastrophic" — barrels through Joplin.

The Joplin Tornado on May 22, 2011 would become the nation's deadliest tornado in 60 years. The damage it caused to Joplin's infrastructure would value $4 billion, but would cost more in the traumatic experiences of survivors and deaths of 158 community members. The 32 minutes of the tornado's lifespan were horrific: employees of Joplin's Pizza Hut watched their store manager get sucked out of the walk-in freezer in which they were sheltering. A nursing home was leveled, with 21 fatalities. Five patients at a local medical center died when power outages deactivated their ventilators.

So, why didn't Joplin residents take the ample warnings seriously?

"When we advance more confident claims and they fail to come to fruition, this constitutes much more powerful evidence against our hypothesis. We can't really blame anyone for losing faith when this occurs."

— Nate Silver, The Signal and the Noise

In the aftermath of the tornado, Joplin residents were frustrated by the perceived lack of clear and urgent warning administered by the National Weather Service. They said, Tornado sirens go off for tests frequently, or even just if dark clouds are forming. How were we supposed to know that this was serious? Forecasters and emergency planners, in response, were disappointed by the lack of urgency by Joplin residents in reacting to tornado sirens. To them, it was human error to blame for the tornado's devastation.

But was it? Weather fatigue is described as the public's general dismissal and distrust of severe weather warnings, fueled by inaccuracies in those warnings in the past. It's the cry wolf effect: if forecasters predict severe weather events, civilians prepare, and nothing happens repeatedly, why should residents bother listening?

That's one side to the argument that the story of the Joplin Tornado of 2011 presents to us. The other, of course, suggests that severe weather warnings are, on average, more accurate than they aren't. So, the overarching question: are severe weather warnings usually followed by those severe weather events? The answer could mitigate the confusion weather fatigue has caused in incidents like Joplin, support pre-emergency infrastructure, and save lives. To answer it, we turn to data.

    Warning Fatigue Is Real

    The data reveals a structural tension in weather warning systems: issuing warnings early enough to be useful means accepting a high false alarm rate. For tornado warnings, 3 in 4 go unverified — not because forecasters are failing, but because the atmosphere is hard to predict. The NWS explicitly trades precision for lead time, accepting false alarms as the cost of giving people those critical extra minutes.

    The consequence is cumulative. Repeated unverified warnings — especially when they cluster in consecutive streaks of 10 or more, as we see in Oklahoma City and Wichita — erode the trust that makes warnings effective. Research on the "cry wolf" effect shows that behavioral compliance drops sharply after experiencing multiple false alarms in a row.

    Understanding these patterns is the first step. Better public communication about why false alarms happen — framing warnings probabilistically rather than as binary predictions — may do more to preserve life-saving trust than simply trying to raise accuracy rates.
