WeatherFatigue Project

Are Storm Warnings
Actually Verified?

Exploring how often severe weather warnings across the United States are confirmed by actual storm reports — and what that means for public trust.

Every year, the National Weather Service issues thousands of severe thunderstorm and tornado warnings. But how many of those warnings are actually confirmed by real-world storm reports?

This project analyzes over 14,000 warnings and 600,000 storm reports from 2008 to 2025, examining false alarm rates, lead times, geographic variation, and long-term trends.

Warning Fatigue

False Alarms
Don't Come Alone

Across 8 forecast offices, we tracked consecutive false alarm streaks — back-to-back warnings with no confirming storm report. These clusters are where fatigue builds.

The Cry Wolf Effect

Research shows that after experiencing multiple false alarms, people take longer to respond to subsequent warnings — or ignore them entirely. In Oklahoma City's forecast area, residents may experience up to 16 consecutive unverified warnings before seeing a real event.

    Warning Fatigue
    Is Real

    The data reveals a structural tension in weather warning systems: issuing warnings early enough to be useful means accepting a high false alarm rate. For tornado warnings, 3 in 4 go unverified — not because forecasters are failing, but because the atmosphere is hard to predict.

    The consequence is cumulative. Repeated unverified warnings — especially when they cluster in consecutive streaks — erode the trust that makes warnings life-saving. People in high-FAR areas become desensitized, slowing their response when a real event arrives.

    Understanding these patterns is the first step. Better communication about why false alarms happen, and how to interpret warnings probabilistically, may be more effective than simply trying to raise accuracy rates.
